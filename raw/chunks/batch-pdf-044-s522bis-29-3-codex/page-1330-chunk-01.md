```text
Reciprocity Without Treaties
A system has been designed to make practical use of this principle.
I
may
say that the idea is not
an original
one; it is in general circulation,
and
I believe that in outline it has
been submitted to public officials.
As it might
prove of interest to national and even international economy, I shall comment upon
it here.
We
are primarily exporters of
raw materials, grain, flaxseed, meats, hides,
wool,
and by-products of the cattle and forest industries.
Let us reduce the value
of these exports to a
common denominator, taking grain as
a unit.
By averaging
the prices for the last few years,
we obtain the following result:
on this basis
meats
are valued at 4 1/2, cattle by-products at 7, forest by-products at 1 1/4.
On multiplying these indices by the physical volume each country imports of each
category,
and adding these results by countries,
we obtain what we might call the
"trade volume."
By way of illustration, let us take this "trade volume" from
September to September and increase the customs duties by 15$.
Then if we establish
for
a period from January to January a discount
on customs duties of 4$ for each
10$ of the "trade volume" exported,
we obtain the following result: England, which
imported 37.52$ of our "trade volume" in 1930, would obtain a discount of 15.02$
on customs duties for 1931; Belgium, 4,53$;
the Netherlands, 4.15$;
Germany, 3.31$,
etc.
Once the principle of the idea is accepted,
the combinations which could be
made
should
be very carefully considered, taking into account the dynamics of the
formula,
the interests of soundly established Argentine industries, and the fact
that it would
be unwise
to change frequently the system of figuring our "trade
volume"
and
the percentage of discount.
Favors
Go Automatically to Best Customers
The
increase in customs duties should be sufficiently high so that goods
from countries buying the least from us would have a considerable handicap in com¬
petition with similar goods from countries buying more from us.
In this way, without
granting special concessions to certain nations, we should favor the country buying
the most
from us, whether it be England,
the United States, or Belgium.
In each country we should appoint a number of efficient commercial agents,
strategically situated among people of influence in the nation.
These agents would
protect
our exports with a view to increasing theirs.
We would, consequently,
establish
among the three or four largest consumers of our products a competition
in the
Durchase of
our raw materials, which would result in improving prices of our
export merchandise and in rapidly liquidating our crops.
When one considers that,
if the United States bought from Argentina only 2 or 3 per cent of the total amount
of beef it
consumes,
the exportation of meat to that country would amount to from
150,000 to 225,000 tons, and the "trade volume" of the United States thereby approxi¬
mate that of England,
one is naturally tempted to place these two great nations in
competition.
In this way countries would increase their purchases from us,
some in
order to improve their position on the scale, and others to maintain their customs
preferences in the market.
Dr.
Enrique Gil.
Note.
— The writer of this letter is a close observer of affairs in Argentina.
The
views here presented are his own and in no sense carry the endorsement of the Council.
```
