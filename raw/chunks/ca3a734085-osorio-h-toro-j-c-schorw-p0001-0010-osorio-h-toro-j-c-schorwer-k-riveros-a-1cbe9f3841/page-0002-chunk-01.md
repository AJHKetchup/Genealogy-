The   foundation   of   the   University,   the   beginnings   of   anatomical   activities   in   1919   and   the   contribution   of   its   main   pioneers,   show   the   difficulties   of   teaching   this discipline   in   Chile.   From   a   historical   perspective,   knowing   within   this   century   of   teaching   the   physical   resources   used   for   cadaveric   dissection,   the   detail   of   the pedagogical   methodologies   used   and   the   anatomical   resources   available,   turn   out   to   be   a   contribution   to   the   knowledge   of   the   development   of   anatomy   in   Chile.

## INTRODUCTION

Concepción  demands   a   University. 'Without   truth   and   effort   there   is no   progress'   (Wilhelm,   1966),   this   phrase   written   in   the   entrance   hall   to the   second   floor   of   the   building   that   welcomed   the   first   year   of   Medicine at   the   University   of   Concepción   in   1924,   located   at   O`Higgins   Street 850,   represents   the   difficulties   faced   by   the   teaching   of   anatomy   in southern   Chile.

At   the   beginning   of   the   20th   century,   the   citizens   of   the   thriving

Concepción   demanded   the   creation   of   a   University. This   initiative   was   supported   by   the   tireless   work   of   a   committee   that advocated   the   joint   creation   of   three   vital   pillars:   a   University,   a   Clinical Hospital   and   a   Faculty   of   Medicine.   The   latter   was   to   be   closely   linked to   the   Hospital.   The   foundation   of   these   institutions,   not   without difficulties,   was   achieved   at   different   times,   the   first   of   which   was   the creation   of   the   University   of   Concepción   in   1919   (Muñoz, 1993).

The   desire   to   develop   university   education   in   the   city   of Concepción   began   in   the   18th   century.   In   this   city,   located   about   500 kilometers   south   of   Santiago,   the   Jesuit   order   founded   the   Universidad Pencopolitana   under   the   auspices   of   the   Bishopric   of   Concepción.   This institution,   which   operated   between   1724   and   1767,   had   the   aspiration of   developing   a   School   of   Medicine,   which   was   cut   short   by   the   violent earthquake   and   tsunami   of   1751.   In   this   catastrophe,   the   city   had   to move   its   location   from   the   current   Penco   to   the   valley   of   Mocha,   located between   the   Biobio   and   Andalien   rivers.   Added   to   this   was   the   expulsion of   the   Jesuits   in   1767,   which   ultimately   postponed   the   creation   of   this School   (Wilhelm,   1962;   Cavieres,   2013).

This   committee   was   made   up   of   a   group   of   illustrious   people from   Concepción   with   a   significant   Masonic   presence,   including   Mr. Enrique   Molina,   Rector   of   the   Men's   High   School   of   Concepción   and   Dr. Virginio   Gómez,   Director   of   the   San   Juan   de   Dios   Hospital   (Da   Costa, 1995).

**   Dr.   Virginio   Gómez   was   born   in   Los   Angeles   in   1877.   Once   he   received   his   medical degree,   he   obtained   a   scholarship   in   1904   to   continue   his   studies   in   Germany   (Luvel, 1968)   (Fig.   1A).   After   a   long   journey   that   included   his   participation   in   the   First   World   War, upon   his   return   to   Chile,   in   1917   he   was   named   Director   of   the   San   Juan   de   Dios   Hospital in   Concepción.   In   1920,   he   was   appointed   Director   of   the   Hospital   San   Juan   de   Dios   in   Concepción.
