                                                                                                11
(Uruguay)

                                        EXHIBIT B

                  Reply of Montevideo Police Department to Questionnaire submitted
                                        June 20, 1924
                  (Translated by Office of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs)

                  1.    There exists a traffic in women, nearly all coming from
                  Europe, and engaged in by well-known international caftens (pimps-
                  souteneurs). But such traffic does not have Uruguay for its final
                  destination, as most of the business is directed to Buenos Aires.
                  Those women arriging in this country as a consequence of the trade
                  in the Argentine capital, come in under conditions radically differ-
                  ent from those affecting women who are deceived and carried from
                  their homes by "maquereaux". Although the women are controlled
                  by such persons, in Uruguay they (women) are already prostitutes
                  with a perfect knowledge of the kind of business they are required
                  to engage in. This circumstances make more difficult the action
                  of the Police, as it finds persons with knowledge of the business
                  and also those who are terrified under compulsion of their exploit-
                  ers. This trade is clandestine.

                  2.    The traffic does not exist in this mamer. Sometimes
                  it happens that prostitutes leave the country, alone or with men
                  that live upon them. They go by preference to Argentine or Brazil.
                  But a real traffic does not exist, and the voyage is made in order
                  to look for better conditions (outlook), but always in the same
                  kind of a life as here.

                  3.    As traffic from abroad to Uruguay does not exist, ex-
                  cept as before stated, - severe steps are taken by the Government
                  to prevent it by adhering to measures taken by other Governments,
                  and by enacting special legislation in that respect.

                        By the Law of June 18, 1920, the Legislative Power gave au-
                  thorization to the Presidency of the Republic to adhere to the In-
                  ternational Convention concerning the repression of White Slave
                  Traffic, held in Paris on May 4, 1910. (Note: On May 21, 1924,
                  the Presidency of the Republic submitted to the Parliament a Bill
                  demanding its authorization to adhere to the International Conven-
                  tion concerning the suppression of the Traffic in Women and Child-
                  ren, concluded at Geneva on Sept. 30, 1921--Note of Foreign Office).

                        On Oct. 20, 1916 there was approved the "Brum Act" concern-
                  ing the repression of proxenetism and other similar offenses, modi-
                  fying previous provisions, indicating who are guilty of these of-
                  fenses, providing punishments (2 to 4 years, 12 to 15 months of
                  imprisonment and 3 to 6 months of the same or an equivalent fine),
                  etc., etc.
