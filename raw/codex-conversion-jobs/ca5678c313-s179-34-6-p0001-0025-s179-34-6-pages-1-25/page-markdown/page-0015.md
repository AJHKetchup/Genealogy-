EXHIBIT C

Montevideo, July 1st, 1924.

Mr. Carlos Ferrando,
Secretary General, Police Department,
Montevideo, Uruguay.

Dear Sir:

I have received your memorandum of June 24th, 1924, giving
answers to seven of the list of ten preliminary questions handed you
by me on June 20th.

I wish to thank you very much, not only on my own behalf,
but also on behalf of the Special Commission of Experts appointed by
the League of Nations to investigate the Traffic in Women and Child-
ren in collaboration with Governments. Your intelligent answers will
prove of great assistance to this International Commission in grappling
with this grave International problem.

I now beg leave to avail myself of the permission already
granted by you at our first interview, of submitting a further list
of questions which arise naturally from a study of your answers to
the first list, and also from my several very helpful conferences
with the officials of the Immigration Service and with the President
and other officials of the National Council of Hygiene. In asking
these questions I will base them on the first list of questions,
numbering each new question with the same number as the question in
the first list on which it is based.

I(a). I understand you to state in answer to my first ques-
tion that, although it is true that alien women are brought by "well
known international caftens" from Europe to Uruguay for purposes of
prostitution, neither the caftens nor their victims remain long in
Uruguay, but proceed to Buenos Aires, which is the center of this
international traffic. If I am correct in my understanding of your
answer, may I further inquire what measures the Uruguayan Government
takes or contemplates taking, either alone or in cooperation with the
Argentine Government to prevent the use by such traffickers of Uruguay
and especially of Montevideo as a way station either by such traffick-
ers or by their women victims?

I(b). In this connection, is it true that such traffickers
and their victims can and do enter Montevideo from Rio de Janeiro by
railroad through Rivera without passports or other papers, and that
many of these individuals, after a short stay in Montevideo proceed,
still without documents, by train to Salto and other border stations,
and cross the river into Argentine at those points by private launch,
easily evading the Uruguayan and Argentine authorities?
