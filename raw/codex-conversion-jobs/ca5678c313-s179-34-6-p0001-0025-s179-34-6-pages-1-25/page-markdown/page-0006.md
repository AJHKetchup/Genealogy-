<u>SUMMARY OF FACTS</u>

It was admitted by the Police Department and corroborated
from other sources that there was in 1924 some International Traffic
in women and girls to Uruguay carried on by "well-known international
caftens" (souteneurs), but that it was small in extent and directed
from Buenos Aires, the ultimate destination of both traffickers and
their victims. In short, Montevideo was but a way station.

It was claimed by the Police Department that International
Traffic was kept at a minimum by the rigorous enforcement of the Brum
Act by the Police (Passed Oct. 20, 1916; <u>See Exhibit N)</u>. *

The Immigration Department stated that women or girls,
traveling alone, (in any class) must have, in addition to passports,
Certificates from the Uruguayan Consuls at points of departure stat-
ing their identities, ages, nationalities, and that they were of good
moral character; that girls under age could not land at all, no mat-
ter what papers they had, unless they came with adult relatives or
guardians authorized in writing by the girls' parents to bring them;
otherwise there is no inspection of passengers in the first class from
whom passports or visas are not required. The Immigration Law applies
only to second and third-class passengers who may land if they are
not diseased, infirm, or known or suspected criminals. As to suspects,
the burden of proof is placed on them to prove within 24 hours that
they are not criminals, otherwise they cannot land. The correctional
Justice of the Peace has final jurisdiction in such matters and his
discretion is absolute.

*[Handwritten in right margin, vertically from bottom to top]*
For other laws & Regulations see Exhibits P. Q. & R.

*[Handwritten footnote at bottom of page]*
* See message of the President of Uruguay to Parliament of
August 13th, 1924 (attached hereto and marked Exhbt O) admitting
the existence of traffic and the inadequacy of this and other existing
laws to prevent it. Also advocates a new law attached.
