He told me that a few months ago a party of sixteen who were being smuggled
across at Paysandu met with an accident. The boat turned over and fourteen
of the sixteen people drowned in the river. Since then people are afraid
to cross through there. He further said that the most he permitted his
boatmen to take over at one time were ten as it was not safe to take more
at one time. He further told me that if they should be stricter at Concordia
against immigrant smuggling it would mean as he expressed "Good by for our
business" and these without passports would have to remain in Uruguay. I
informed him that should my two women not show up the following day (Saturday)
on the train from Montevideo I would return in the evening to see what
happened. He suggested that I pay his fare to Montevideo and that he
would go there to bring the women as I may get into trouble. I insisted
that I go myself.

The following morning Isaac Yukelson called at the hotel early
and Berenstein, Yukelson and I went for a walk. During the course of
conversation Berenstein told me his life history and how he became bankrupt.
He said that he failed for 500,000 Pesos about three years. That he run
away for some 150,000 Pesos and spent some time in Paraguay. He recently
returned to Argentine and thinking that the limitation period of three
years had expired and the authorities could not touch only to find that
a new law was enacted to cover cases like his. When he heard that the
Police were looking for him he run away to Salto. He invested his money
there with a bank which brings him 8% interest.

Yukelson and I met the 11 a.m. train from Montevideo. There
were two Russian immigrants who were approached by Yukelson who took them
home with him. I informed Yukelson that I would return the same evening
for Montevideo.
