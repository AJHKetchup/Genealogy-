```markdown
The explosion of such large quantities of high explosives invariably produces a very high temperature; the effect of the explosion is generally enhanced by fire.

But the effect of highly explosive bombs becomes still more terrible when they carry delay-fuses. The German Red Cross has learned that bombs have been invented which bore themselves deep into the earth and do not explode until after the elapse of 4, 6, 8, 12, 18, 24 and 36 hours.

It is obvious that all measures of rescue or protection must fail of their object if such a bomb does not explode until hours or even days have elapsed. In these circumstances, moreover, the best organized rescue corps will be unable to act, inasmuch as it would constitute practically a useless sacrifice of human life to send them to the

spot.

The German Red Cross has noticed with particular interest an article published by the Swedish officer, R. Lindblad, in the 'Ny Militàr Tidskrift" for January, 1928, in which he writes: - 'The effect of mine-bombs is much more to be feared that the effect of gas-bombs, against which effective protection may be provided with comparative ease."

In 'La France Militaire" of March 27, 1928, General Rouquerol publishes an article entitled 'The Extension of War Dangers", in which he says: 'The League of Nations has expressed the platonic desire that all countries should prohibit the bombarding of civilian populations with poison-gas bombs. A German professor, in denouncing attacks made far behind the lines, has declared that only the fighting troops should be fired on. In reality, all these wishes are preached to the winds.

Public opinion sees the greatest terror of air bombardments in the use of poison gases. In point of fact, there is nothing to prove that gas bombardments will be more dangerous than bombardments with explosives. The chief danger lies in the increased

efficiency of military aviation.

'One can well imagine that the victories of an army may be stultified by the breakdown of the civilian

population of the hinterland."

The German Red Cross has carefully studied the question of what can be clone to create effective protection and to banish the dangers described above. The German Red Cross has come to the conclusion that measures adopted to alleviate more or less the sufferings caused to the population by a possible air attack must all remain, in certain respects, ineffective. In considering the question of what can be done to create an efficacious remedy, the German Red Cross inclines to the view that declarations on the part of individual states not to resort to chemical warfare unless and until the enemy shall have done so would be of inestimable value. True, this would be only a relatively imperfect attempt to indicate the direction in which a really satisfactory solution is to be found. The initial imperfection of the present proposal is to be seen in the fact that no clear distinction can be made between chemical and other bombs.
```
