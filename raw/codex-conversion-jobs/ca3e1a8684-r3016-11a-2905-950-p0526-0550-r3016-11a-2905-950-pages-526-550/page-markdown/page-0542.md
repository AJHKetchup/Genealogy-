The German Red Cross and many other associations and private persons in Germany have watched, with the keenest interest, the deliberations and decisions of the International Commission of Experts which the International Committee of the Red Cross kindly invited to meet at Brussels from the 14 th.-18 th. January 1928, to consider means for protecting the civilian population against chemical attacks in warfare. The German Red Cross had the honour of sending two delegates to the deliberations, namely, Herr von Moellendorff, formerly State Secretary, and Dr. Hanslian. The German Red Cross having given the most careful consideration to the matters discussed at Brussels and to the questions involved therein, takes the liberty of submitting herewith a few suggestions to the Xlllth. International Conference of the Red Cross.

The International Conference of Experts, in drawing up their resolutions, adopted the standpoint that, in any future war, the entire civilian population of the countries affected will be in danger of being attacked with chemicals and by means of aeroplanes. (I. II. 1.)

This means that protective measures are requisite everywhere; it cannot mean that special measures are not necessary for the protection of the masses of mankind crowded together in big cities, industrial centres and such like places. The arrangments for alarming the population, the construction of dug-outs, the provision of gas-masks, the installation of ambulance centres and other measures recommended by the Brussels Conference are matters of importance, but they do not ensure

adequate protection.

Against what must protection be provided? Against air-bombs of every description. It cannot be objected that, in the large cities of the fighting-zone proper, even gas-clouds or gas-bombs may give rise to all the terrors of chemical warfare.
