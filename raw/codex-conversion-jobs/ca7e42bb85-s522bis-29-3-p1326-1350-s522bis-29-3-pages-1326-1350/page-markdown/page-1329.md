## Not for Publication.

## LETTER FROM ARGENTINA

## THE NEED OF EXPORT OUTLETS

Unfortunately, the predictions which we ventured to make in previous correspondence are gradually coming true. During the past three years the value of American goods imported into Argentina has declined to less than one-tenth, or less than the value in 1910. In 1929 Argentina imported from the United States merchan¬ dise valued at $227,000,000 gold, and exported to that country goods valued at $94,000,000 gold, with the peso at par or almost at par. During the first ten months of 1932, according to figures of the Department of Commerce in Washington, Argentina bought merchandise valued at $13,000,000 gold from the United States.

This is a result of the Smoot-Hawley Tariff, which has complicated the economic and financial difficulties from which both countries are suffering. Argen¬ tina has stopped buying because it has no money with which to pay, and it has no money, as La Prensa has very well said, because the United States has closed its ports to Argentine products and to the European countries that were Argentina's best clients in the purchase of grain, meats, hides and wool. This, in addition to the fact that Argentina uses a large percentage of its foreign exchange to continue paying the service on its national debt in dollars.

## Decline of Trade with the United States

The disappearance of the favorable trade balance for the United States of 133,000,000 gold pesos not only affects the American manufacturer, but to a certain extent increases unemployment, and causes losses to the United States merchant marine. 7/e wonder, therefore, what advantage the United States has obtained from its agrarian protectionism, especially what benefit the American farmer has derived from it.

Incidentally, the flaxseed which Argentina sold to the United States in 1929, valued at more than $40,000,000, is now practically excluded by a duty which amounts to nine paper pesos per kilo. The importation of Argentine meat is pro¬ hibited on the pretext of avoiding the spread of the Foot and Mouth Disease, even though it has been proved, first, that the freezing of meat eliminates any such danger, and secondly, that if such a danger existed, the inhabitants of the British Isles who have consumed Argentine meats for years would have been decimated.

In 1932 Great Britain purchased 34.5$ of our exports, and the United States only 3.4$. It is not surprising, therefore, that Argentine public opinion is being influenced by campaigns in favor of the principle, "buy from those who buy from us."

This was a phrase Which the Argentine Rural Society launched in 1926; it crossed the ocean, being answered with the argument of triangular compensation of international trade; and it was commented upon in Argentina as follows; "Beware! the one who buys the most from us is the Argentine workman." In practice, however, it remained nothing more than a general aspiration, crystallized in a synthetic, euphonious phrase. In reality it is only the old formula: "do ut des.'*
