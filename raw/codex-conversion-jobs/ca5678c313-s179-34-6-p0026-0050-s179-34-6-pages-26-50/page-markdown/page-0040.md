```
Those rules are nothing more than the extension, for the
guidance of magistrates, of the rational principle laid down
in Article 301 of the Code of Criminal Procedure, which does not
offend against the public sense of justice because it in no way
deprives the accused of his essential rights.
        The extension of this principle, together with the legal
recognition of presumptions- both of them measures whose sole
purpose is to allow the use in criminal law of classes of
evidence which are already admitted in international conventions
to which this country has adhered- will enable magistrates who
have formed a personal conviction of the guilt of the accused
to pronounce a sentence, which the excessive rigidity of the
rules of procedure at present prevents them from doing.
        The reform of the law of 1916 would, however, be incomplete,
and would only admit of the partial observance of the
obligations contracted in international conventions which are
already part of Uruguayan law, if these main principles, the
fruit of eight years' experience, were not supplemented by
others of an even more important nature-not to speak of
amendments introduced to make the Penal Code consistent by the
suppression of clauses in the law on procuring which apply to
offences already penalised, such as abduction and rape. The
object of these further measures is to attack the evil in the
very centres where it develops and thrives.
```
